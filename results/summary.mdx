The Summary is where you go first after analysis finishes. It shows what you learned, what participants agreed on, where they disagreed, and what to do about it.

<Frame>
  [IMAGE: The full Summary view showing the overview section, metrics cards, and findings]
</Frame>

## Overview and metrics

At the top, you'll see key numbers:

- **Participant count** — total across all sessions
- **Session count** — how many sessions ran
- **Consensus rate** — percentage of topics where participants broadly agreed
- **Debates** — topics where participants had opposing views

<Frame>
  [IMAGE: The metrics cards row showing participant count, session count, consensus rate, and debates count]
</Frame>

---

## Decisions

For each research objective, Convo generates a **decision card**:

- **Verdict** — a clear conclusion (e.g., "Participants strongly prefer Option A")
- **Confidence** — High, Moderate, or Low, based on how consistent the evidence is
- **Evidence** — data points supporting the verdict
- **Pulls** — factors attracting participants toward a position
- **Pushes** — factors pushing them away
- **Key quotes** — direct quotes illustrating the finding
- **Recommendations** — suggested next steps

<Frame>
  [IMAGE: A single decision card showing the verdict, confidence level, evidence, pulls/pushes, and quotes]
</Frame>

<Tip>
  Low confidence decisions usually mean you need more data or a follow-up study on that topic.
</Tip>

---

## Key findings

Below the decisions, you'll find findings that cut across your objectives. Each one includes a clear statement, the number of participants who expressed it, and supporting quotes.

These are your headline insights — the stuff you'd put in a research report or stakeholder deck.

---

## Agreements

Topics where participants reached consensus. Convo spots these by looking for patterns where most people said similar things.

Each one shows the consensus point, how many participants aligned, and representative quotes.

<Frame>
  [IMAGE: The agreements section showing consensus points with participant counts and quotes]
</Frame>

---

## Debates

Topics where participants genuinely disagreed. Each debate shows:

- **Side A** — one position, with supporting participants and quotes
- **Side B** — the other position, with its supporters and quotes

These tend to be the most useful outputs from focus groups. They show you real tensions and trade-offs in how your audience thinks.

<Frame>
  [IMAGE: The debates section showing a contested topic with Side A and Side B positions]
</Frame>

---

## Emergent insights

Sometimes participants bring up things you didn't plan for. Convo flags these as **emergent insights** — stuff that came up organically and wasn't in your guide.

Worth paying attention to. They can reveal blind spots or opportunities you hadn't considered.
